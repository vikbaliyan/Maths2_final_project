Abstract¶

Problem:

The rapid spread of COVID-19 and pneumonia has necessitated the development of efficient and accurate diagnostic tools. Both diseases present similar symptoms and can be detected through medical imaging, particularly chest X-rays and CT scans. Manual interpretation of these images is time-consuming and requires specialized expertise. The problem addressed in this study is the development of an automated system capable of classifying COVID-19 and pneumonia images, thus aiding in the timely and accurate diagnosis of both diseases.

Approach:

To tackle this problem, a combination of custom-built Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) and fine-tuned pre-trained models (VGG16 and MobileNetV2) were employed. The models were trained on a publicly available dataset containing COVID-19 and pneumonia image data sourced from Kaggle. The approach involved preprocessing the images, including resizing, normalization, and data augmentation, followed by the construction and training of the models. The training process was carefully monitored, and various hyperparameters were tuned to optimize performance

Results:

The custom CNN model and the fine-tuned VGG16 and MobileNetV2 models demonstrated promising results in classifying the COVID-19 and pneumonia images. The models achieved satisfactory accuracy levels, showcasing their potential as valuable tools in the diagnostic process for both diseases. The results indicate that deep learning models can effectively assist healthcare professionals in the rapid and accurate diagnosis of COVID-19 and pneumonia, thereby contributing to timely medical intervention.

Introduction¶

The emergence of COVID-19 has posed unprecedented challenges to healthcare systems worldwide. Alongside COVID-19, pneumonia continues to be a significant health concern, often presenting similar symptoms and complications. Both diseases can lead to severe respiratory distress and require prompt diagnosis and treatment.

Medical imaging, particularly chest X-rays and CT scans, has become an essential tool in the diagnosis of both COVID-19 and pneumonia. These images provide valuable insights into the condition of the lungs and can reveal characteristic patterns associated with each disease. However, manual interpretation of these images is a complex and time-consuming task that requires specialized expertise. Moreover, the high demand for diagnostic imaging during the pandemic has further strained the available resources.

In response to these challenges, there has been a growing interest in leveraging artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML) techniques to automate the image analysis process. This study aims to develop and evaluate deep learning models capable of classifying COVID-19 and pneumonia images. The models are trained on a publicly available dataset containing COVID-19 and pneumonia image data sourced from Kaggle.

The introduction of automated image classification systems could revolutionize the way healthcare professionals approach the diagnosis of COVID-19 and pneumonia. Related Work¶

The application of deep learning in medical imaging has been a subject of extensive research, particularly in the context of diagnosing respiratory diseases like COVID-19 and pneumonia. This section highlights some of the significant contributions and methodologies that have shaped the current landscape of this research area.

COVID-19 Imaging: Early in the COVID-19 pandemic, researchers recognized the potential of AI in detecting the disease from chest X-rays and CT scans. Wang et al. (2020) developed a deep learning model to identify COVID-19 features in CT images, achieving promising results. Similarly, Apostolopoulos and Mpesiana (2020) utilized transfer learning with pre-trained CNNs to classify COVID-19 X-rays.

Pneumonia Detection: Prior to the COVID-19 pandemic, deep learning had already been applied to pneumonia detection. Rajpurkar et al. (2018) introduced a dataset of chest X-rays and a model that outperformed radiologists in detecting pneumonia. Kermany et al. (2018) also employed CNNs to diagnose pneumonia from chest X-rays, demonstrating the effectiveness of deep learning in this domain.

Multi-Class Classification: Some studies have focused on multi-class classification, distinguishing between COVID-19, pneumonia, and normal cases. For example, Khan et al. (2020) proposed a model that classifies X-rays into these three categories, emphasizing the importance of differentiating COVID-19 from other respiratory illnesses.

Transfer Learning and Pre-Trained Models: The use of transfer learning and pre-trained models like VGG16 and MobileNetV2 has become common in medical imaging. These models, initially trained on large datasets like ImageNet, have been fine-tuned for specific medical tasks, often yielding robust performance (Tajbakhsh et al., 2020).

Data Augmentation and Preprocessing: The challenge of limited labeled data in medical imaging has led to the exploration of data augmentation techniques. Pertinent studies have shown that augmenting the data with rotations, flips, and other transformations can enhance model performance (Shorten et al., 2019).

Interpretability and Explainability: As deep learning models are increasingly adopted in clinical settings, the need for interpretability has grown. Researchers are working on methods to make model decisions more transparent and understandable to medical professionals (Carvalho et al., 2019).

Challenges and Ethical Considerations: Alongside the technical advancements, researchers have also addressed challenges related to data privacy, model bias, and ethical considerations in AI-driven medical diagnosis (Wiens et al., 2019).

In summary, the related work underscores the rapid advancements and diverse approaches in applying deep learning to diagnose COVID-19 and pneumonia. This study builds upon these foundations, aiming to contribute to the ongoing efforts to enhance diagnostic accuracy and efficiency through AI.

Preprocessing techniques are applied to standardize the images. Ethical Considerations:

The dataset is composed of anonymized images, and all identifiable patient information has been removed to comply with privacy regulations. Dataset Source:

Methods¶

1.

Model Architecture

The project involves the creation of a custom CNN model and the fine-tuning of pre-trained models like VGG16 and MobileNetV2. Below are the details of the architectures:

Custom CNN Model

Convolutional Layers: Three convolutional layers with 32, 64, and 128 filters, respectively, and a kernel size of (3, 3). ReLU activation functions are used.

Max-Pooling Layers: Following each convolutional layer, a max-pooling layer with a pool size of (2, 2) is applied.

Flattening Layer: The output is flattened to feed into the dense layers.

Dense Layers: A dense layer with 512 units and ReLU activation, followed by a dropout layer with a rate of 0.5 to reduce overfitting.

Output Layer: A dense layer with 3 units (corresponding to 3 classes) and a softmax activation function.

Fine-Tuning Pre-Trained Models

VGG16: The VGG16 model is loaded with pre-trained weights. The classifier is modified to match the number of classes in the dataset. MobileNetV2: Similar to VGG16, MobileNetV2 is also loaded with pre-trained weights and fine-tuned for the specific task.

2. Compilation

Optimizer: The Adam optimizer is used for the custom CNN model, VGG16, and MobileNetV2.

Loss Function: Categorical cross-entropy is used as the loss function.

Metrics: Accuracy is chosen as the evaluation metric to monitor the performance of the models.

3. Data Preprocessing

Rescaling: Pixel values are normalized between 0 and 1.

Data Augmentation: To enhance the model's performance and prevent overfitting, data augmentation techniques are applied, including rotation, width and height shifting, shear transformation, zooming, and horizontal flipping.

Image Resizing: Images are resized to 150x150 pixels to maintain consistency.

VGG16 Model fine tuning

import torch
import torchvision
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from torchvision.transforms import Compose, ToTensor, Resize, CenterCrop
from torch.utils.data import DataLoader
from torchvision.datasets import ImageFolder
from torchvision.models import vgg16

# Check if GPU is available
device = torch.device("cuda" if torch.cuda.is_available() else "cpu")

# Data preparation and transformation
def load_and_resize_image(image, size=(256, 256)):
    image = torchvision.transforms.functional.resize(image, size)
    return image.to(device)

transform = Compose([ToTensor(), load_and_resize_image])
train_dataset = ImageFolder('train', transform=transform)
train_loader = DataLoader(train_dataset, batch_size=4, shuffle=True)

# Load the VGG-16 model
model = vgg16(pretrained=True)

# Modify the model to match the number of classes in your dataset
num_classes = len(train_dataset.classes)
model.classifier[-1] = torch.nn.Linear(4096, num_classes)

# Move the model to the GPU if available
if torch.cuda.is_available():
    model = model.to(device)

# Set up the optimizer and the learning rate scheduler
params = [p for p in model.parameters() if p.requires_grad]
optimizer = torch.optim.SGD(params, lr=0.001, momentum=0.9, weight_decay=0.0005)
lr_scheduler = torch.optim.lr_scheduler.StepLR(optimizer, step_size=3, gamma=0.1)

# Define the loss function
criterion = torch.nn.CrossEntropyLoss()

# Fine-tune the model
num_epochs = 5  # You can adjust this according to your needs

losses = []
accuracies = []

for epoch in range(num_epochs):
    model.train()
    running_loss = 0.0
    correct_predictions = 0
    total_predictions = 0

    for images, labels in train_loader:
        images, labels = images.to(device), labels.to(device)
        outputs = model(images)
        loss = criterion(outputs, labels)

        optimizer.zero_grad()
        loss.backward()
        optimizer.step()

        running_loss += loss.item()
        _, predicted = torch.max(outputs, 1)
        total_predictions += labels.size(0)
        correct_predictions += (predicted == labels).sum().item()

    epoch_loss = running_loss / len(train_loader)
    epoch_accuracy = correct_predictions / total_predictions
    losses.append(epoch_loss)
    accuracies.append(epoch_accuracy)

    lr_scheduler.step()

    print(f"Epoch {epoch+1}/{num_epochs}, Loss: {epoch_loss:.4f}, Accuracy: {epoch_accuracy:.4f}")

# Save the fine-tuned model as .h5
torch.save(model.state_dict(), 'vgg16.pth')

# Plot Loss and Accuracy Graphs
plt.figure(figsize=(10, 5))
plt.subplot(1, 2, 1)
plt.plot(range(1, num_epochs + 1), losses, 'b-')
plt.xlabel('Epochs')
plt.ylabel('Loss')
plt.title('Training Loss')

plt.subplot(1, 2, 2)
plt.plot(range(1, num_epochs + 1), accuracies, 'r-')
plt.xlabel('Epochs')
plt.ylabel('Accuracy')
plt.title('Training Accuracy')

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

import torch
import torchvision
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from torchvision.transforms import Compose, ToTensor, Resize, CenterCrop
from torch.utils.data import DataLoader
from torchvision.datasets import ImageFolder
from torchvision.models import mobilenet_v2, vgg16

# Data preparation and transformation
def load_and_resize_image(image, size=(256, 256)):
    return torchvision.transforms.functional.resize(image, size)

transform = Compose([ToTensor(), load_and_resize_image])
test_dataset = ImageFolder('test', transform=transform)
test_loader = DataLoader(test_dataset, batch_size=4, shuffle=False)

# Load the saved MobileNetV2 model
model_mobilenet = mobilenet_v2(pretrained=False)
num_classes = len(test_dataset.classes)
model_mobilenet.classifier[1] = torch.nn.Linear(1280, num_classes)
model_mobilenet.load_state_dict(torch.load('mobilenet_v2.pth'))
model_mobilenet.eval()

# Load the saved VGG16 model
model_vgg16 = vgg16(pretrained=False)
num_classes = len(test_dataset.classes)
model_vgg16.classifier[-1] = torch.nn.Linear(4096, num_classes)
model_vgg16.load_state_dict(torch.load('vgg16.pth'))
model_vgg16.eval()

# Evaluate the models on the test dataset
def evaluate_model(model):
    correct_predictions = 0
    total_predictions = 0
    total_loss = 0.0
    criterion = torch.nn.CrossEntropyLoss()

    with torch.no_grad():
        for images, labels in test_loader:
            outputs = model(images)
            loss = criterion(outputs, labels)
            total_loss += loss.item()

            _, predicted = torch.max(outputs, 1)
            total_predictions += labels.size(0)
            correct_predictions += (predicted == labels).sum().item()

    accuracy = correct_predictions / total_predictions
    average_loss = total_loss / len(test_loader)
    return accuracy, average_loss

accuracy_mobilenet, loss_mobilenet = evaluate_model(model_mobilenet)
accuracy_vgg16, loss_vgg16 = evaluate_model(model_vgg16)

print("MobileNetV2 Accuracy:", accuracy_mobilenet)
print("MobileNetV2 Average Loss:", loss_mobilenet)
print("VGG16 Accuracy:", accuracy_vgg16)
print("VGG16 Average Loss:", loss_vgg16)

# Plot Loss and Accuracy Comparison Graphs
models = ['MobileNetV2', 'VGG16']
accuracies = [accuracy_mobilenet, accuracy_vgg16]
losses = [loss_mobilenet, loss_vgg16]

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 5))

plt.bar(models, accuracies, color=['blue', 'green'])
plt.ylabel('Accuracy')
plt.title('Accuracy Comparison')

plt.subplot(1, 2, 2)
plt.bar(models, losses, color=['blue', 'green'])
plt.ylabel('Loss')
plt.title('Loss Comparison')

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
